There is a question I would ask anyone hiring a lighting designer for the first time.

Do you think lighting a room is a matter of <em>picking pretty fixtures and
putting them on the ceiling</em>? Or do you actually see — and care — that
each fixture has a specific behavior, and that the behavior, more than the
fixture itself, decides what the room <em>feels like</em> to be in?

The honest answer separates good clients from the rest. Because lighting,
when it is taken seriously, is the most quietly powerful tool in residential
design. It is also the one most often treated as decoration.

This project let me make the argument the way I prefer to make it — through
the work itself.

## The brief: comfort and flexibility

The clients had two requirements. They were specific, and they were the right
ones.

The first was <strong>comfort</strong>. They wanted the social areas of the
home to be well lit, but never aggressive. No glare. No fixtures that you
notice when you sit on the sofa. No surfaces blown out and no corners left in
shadow. The kind of lighting you stop seeing because the room simply works.

The second was <strong>flexibility</strong>. The social area is fully
integrated — living, dining, kitchen, gourmet zone, and the outdoor pool deck
all read as one continuous space when the glass doors open. They needed a
lighting plan that could go from <em>fully lit and active</em> to
<em>cinematic and intimate</em>, and to half a dozen states in between.

Two requirements. Both technical. Both about the body of the person in the room.

## The first move: hide the source

The most important decision in this project was to put light <em>inside the
millwork</em>.

The walls already carried wood paneling — handsome on its own. We let it do
double duty by integrating concealed linear LED profiles into the joinery,
washing the wall from above and below. The fixture disappears. The light
remains.

This single decision — <em>indirect light from a hidden source</em> — does
two things at once. It lifts the ambient illuminance to a level the room
genuinely needs without ever putting a glare source in the line of sight.
And it makes the wood glow from within, instead of being lit from the front.
The paneling becomes the lamp.

The choice was confirmed in <strong>Dialux</strong> with a full photometric
calculation: the right luminous flux, the right beam distribution, the right
spacing along the run. A walkthrough of that calculation will live at the
top of this post once I record it.

## The ceiling: a vocabulary of beam angles

The ceiling — a beautiful cumaru wood plane — carries the second layer.
Recessed fixtures, every one of them chosen to do exactly one job.

A <strong>12° beam angle</strong> for tight, focused pools of light over the
dining table and over the artwork on the feature wall. These are the
emphases. They create the cinematic version of the room.

A <strong>48° beam angle</strong> for the kitchen prep zones and the lounge
seating. Wider distribution, softer falloff, closer to ambient. This is what
you turn on when you are using the room.

An <strong>80° beam angle</strong> for the perimeter and the circulation paths.
This is the layer that fills the room without claiming attention. It is what
makes a 1,500 sq ft open plan feel <em>lit</em> at low intensity.

Three beam angles. Three jobs. The dimmer, more than the switches, decides
what the room is at any given moment.

## The non-negotiables: 2700K, CRI 95, low UGR

Three numbers run underneath every fixture in this project, and I will not
specify a residential lighting plan without them.

<strong>CCT 2700K.</strong> The warm end of the residential range. A degree
of warmth that complements wood without pushing into yellow. Not 3000K — 3000
is for kitchens that read as workspaces. 2700 is for rooms you live in after dark.

<strong>CRI 95.</strong> Color Rendering Index measures how accurately a
light source renders colors compared to natural daylight. Below CRI 90, your
materials will fight you — the walnut goes muddy, the brass goes dull, the
skin tones go strange. CRI 95 is the floor for high-end residential. There
is no substitute.

<strong>Low UGR.</strong> Unified Glare Rating — the parameter I evaluate
most carefully, and the one most often ignored. UGR is what determines
whether a fixture lights the room or <em>punishes the eyes</em>. Standard
fixtures, even from reputable brands, often score badly here. Specifying
low-UGR optics is the difference between a room you can sit in for four
hours and a room that quietly fatigues you.

These three numbers do not show up in renderings. They show up in how the
room feels to live in over years.

## What Dialux gives me that an instinct cannot

I do not specify residential lighting on instinct anymore. I specify on
calculation, then refine on instinct.

Dialux is a photometric simulation environment. It models how light will
actually behave in a space — how the photons leave the fixture, bounce off
the materials with their real reflectance values, and accumulate into
illuminance levels at every surface. It produces <strong>false-color
visualizations</strong> that show, before anything is installed, exactly
where the room will be over-lit and where it will be too dark.

What this means in practice: every fixture count, every beam angle, every
mounting height in this project was chosen because the calculation said so —
not because it looked right on paper. The space ended up adapting to its
many uses with elegance and intention because the math allowed me to design
for the math.

That is the work. Designing for results that are real and precise, instead
of for guesses dressed up as decisions.

## The argument

A house is not a stage. It is the place a family lives a decade of their
lives in. Light, in that house, is not decoration. It is the medium through
which every other design decision gets seen.

If your lighting designer cannot tell you what the UGR of your living room
fixture is — without looking it up — keep interviewing.
