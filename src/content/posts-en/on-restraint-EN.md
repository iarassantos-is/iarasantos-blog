There is a kind of project I keep returning to when I want to recalibrate
my own work.

Vincent Van Duysen's residential interiors are difficult to look at quickly.
At first glance, the rooms look almost empty — pale plaster, unadorned
volumes, a single piece of furniture that holds the room. The instinct is
to read it as minimalism and move on.

That instinct is wrong. What Van Duysen practices is not minimalism. It is
<em>restraint</em>. The two words are not synonyms, and the difference is
the lesson.

I want to think out loud about that here — and about one place where, even
in work this considered, I would push the conversation a step further.

## What works: the proportional logic of quiet rooms

The first thing to register, in any Van Duysen interior, is that nothing
is arbitrary.

The window is the size it needs to be. The threshold between rooms is the
exact width that lets the eye complete the connection without forcing it.
The ceiling height is calibrated to the volume below it. The doorway aligns
with the centerline of the room beyond. There is a <em>geometric argument</em>
running underneath every photograph of these interiors, and the argument is
what makes the room feel inevitable instead of staged.

This is the part of his work that is least discussed and most worth studying.
You can copy a plaster wall. You cannot copy proportion.

## What works: the material vocabulary kept honest

The palettes are narrow on purpose. Lime plaster, smoke-toned oak, raw stone,
linen, leather aged to a color you cannot order from a swatch. Each material
is allowed to be itself. There is no faux finish, no decorative paint trick
disguising one material as another. The plaster looks like plaster. The wood
looks like wood.

This is what I mean when I write about <em>material truth</em>.
A surface that admits what it is reads more luxurious than any number of
applied finishes pretending to be something they are not. Van Duysen's rooms
are case studies in that argument.

## What works: the discipline of leaving things out

Restraint is not the absence of decisions. It is the result of having made
ten more decisions than the visible result, and having taken nine of them out.

That is the version of minimalism that earns the word elegant. It is not
about owning fewer objects. It is about a designer with the confidence to
leave a wall empty when the wall is the point.

This is also the part of the practice most easily faked and most often
done badly. A bare room without proportional discipline is not restraint —
it is poverty of vision pretending to be sophistication. The difference is
visible in seconds, even if it takes years to articulate why.

## What I would reconsider: the lighting layer

Now the part that is harder to write, because Van Duysen's houses are
already among the most considered residential interiors of the last two
decades. But I will write it anyway, because this is the conversation I
would actually have if I were sitting at a table with him.

The houses are designed almost entirely <em>around natural daylight</em>.
This is a strength — most residential design under-respects the sun and
the building's orientation, and Van Duysen does not. The volumes are
shaped to accept light. The materials are chosen to register it.

But the <em>after-dark plan</em>, in many of the published projects, leans
heavily on a single decorative fixture per room. A floor lamp. A table lamp.
Sometimes a pendant. Beautiful, often vintage, almost always undersized for
the volume.

The published photography, for which the work is justly celebrated, shows
these rooms in perfect daylight. What the photography does not show is what
the room is on a Tuesday in February at 9 p.m., when the only available
light source is one lamp in a corner.

A layered lighting plan — concealed cove for ambient, recessed task light
where the eye actually works, accent on the materials that deserve it after
dark — would not compromise the restraint. Done right, it would extend it.
The ambient layer is invisible by design. The task layer is functional and
unobtrusive. The accent picks up the same plaster, oak, and stone that the
daylight already loves, and continues their conversation past sunset.

This is not a critique of Van Duysen. It is a note about the residential
profession as a whole. We design beautifully for the photograph. We
sometimes forget that the room has to work at midnight too.

## The take-away

Restraint is a discipline. It is the willingness to make ten decisions and
publish one of them. It is the proportional intelligence that makes the
empty wall feel inevitable.

It is also, in residential work, incomplete without a lighting layer that
respects the same logic. The same restraint that earned the wall its
emptiness should earn the room its night.
